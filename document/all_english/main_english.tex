\section{Introduction}

Particle detection is one of the experimental pillars of modern physics. The physical information associated with a microscopic process is not obtained directly, but through the traces left by particles as they travel through matter: ionisation, excitation, scintillation, charge displacement, heat production, or the emission of secondary radiation. Depending on the observable to be prioritised and on the experimental conditions of each problem, very different detector families have been developed. Silicon detectors currently dominate environments requiring very high occupancy tolerance and excellent spatial resolution; calorimeters make it possible to measure the energy and topology of jets or showers; and detectors based on gases and noble media remain particularly competitive when large active volumes, low material budgets, good imaging capabilities and reasonable cost per unit area are required. In this landscape, the state of the art is not defined by a single technology, but by the coexistence of highly specialised solutions optimised for different instrumental needs.

\subsection{Gaseous Detectors}

Within this ecosystem, gaseous detectors occupy a singular position. Their operating principle is conceptually simple: a charged particle or ionising radiation deposits energy in the gas, creates electron--ion pairs and excited states, and the signal is formed from either the transport of those charges or the light emitted during excitation and avalanche development. Behind this simple idea, however, lies a considerable instrumental richness. Gas composition, pressure, detector geometry and electric field allow drift, diffusion, gain, the \textit{attachment} rate and light production to be finely tuned. For this reason, gaseous detectors remain a reference technology for tracking, particle identification, neutrino physics, rare-process searches and large-volume detectors \cite{gonzlezdaz2017gaseous,hayrapetyan2024dune,next2014present,esfahani2023project}.

Historically, the evolution of gaseous detectors can be understood as a transition from macroscopic geometries, such as wire or proportional chambers, to increasingly fine and controlled multiplication structures. This evolution has responded to a very specific need: reducing the characteristic avalanche length in order to improve stability, granularity, rate capability and spatial resolution. In this context, \emph{micro-pattern gaseous detectors} (MPGDs) emerged as one of the most influential families in contemporary gaseous instrumentation \cite{shekhtman2002micropattern,buzulutskov2007radiation}. Their importance lies not only in having replaced earlier technologies in many settings, but also in having opened an entirely new path towards combining electron multiplication, fine imaging and, increasingly, optical readout.

MPGDs comprise devices in which amplification takes place in accurately manufactured microstructures: holes, meshes or metallic patterns with sub-millimetre dimensions. The best-known examples include GEMs and their thicker variants, such as THGEMs, together with other microstructured multipliers designed to operate over different ranges of pressure, gain and robustness. The fundamental advantage of these geometries is that they concentrate intense electric fields in very small, well-defined regions. As a result, the avalanche is more localised, the response becomes more uniform, the discharge probability can be better controlled and the spatial granularity is markedly improved with respect to more classical gaseous detectors \cite{shekhtman2002micropattern,buzulutskov2007radiation,azevedo2016thgem}. In addition, the modular and planar manufacture of many MPGDs facilitates their scaling to large areas and their integration with either segmented electronics or optical readout systems.

From an instrumental point of view, the advantages of MPGDs are particularly attractive in three respects. First, they allow operation at high rates with highly localised signal patterns, which is essential in high-occupancy experiments or whenever fine reconstruction of event topologies is pursued. This is not merely a generic laboratory advantage, but one of the reasons why MPGDs have been adopted in real large-scale detectors. The COMPASS GEMs demonstrated precise tracking in high-particle-flux regions; the ALICE TPC upgrade replaced the classical proportional readout with GEM stacks to enable continuous-readout operation at the Run 3 and Run 4 interaction rates; and the ATLAS New Small Wheel system uses resistive Micromegas in a high-background region to provide precision tracking and triggering capability in the muon spectrometer \cite{ketzer2004compass,adolfsson2021alice,manthos2019atlas}. These examples show that MPGDs are not merely a promising technology, but a mature solution wherever granularity, stability and high-rate operation must be combined. Second, they offer great versatility in the choice of gas mixture and geometry, allowing the detector to be adapted to very different objectives: maximum gain, minimum diffusion, intense scintillation or good timing resolution. Third, MPGDs are naturally compatible with hybrid readout, in which the same avalanche can be observed simultaneously as charge and as light. This duality is one of the reasons why they have evolved from simple charge multipliers into highly versatile platforms for optical TPCs, electroluminescence detectors and advanced three-dimensional reconstruction concepts \cite{oliveira2012simulation,williams2024fatgems}.

\subsection{Scintillation in Gaseous Detectors}

The use of scintillation in MPGDs follows precisely this idea: an avalanche does not merely multiply electrons, but also excites atomic and molecular species capable of emitting photons. The optical signal offers advantages that are not redundant with charge readout. First, it partially decouples the amplification stage from the readout stage: information can be collected using cameras, PMTs or SiPMs located outside the high-field region, reducing constraints associated with cabling and capacitance. Second, optical imaging can provide very high granularity over extended areas, particularly when two-dimensional sensors are employed. Third, light naturally integrates spatial, temporal and topological information: when combined with drift time or fast sensors, it makes it possible to reconstruct tracks, distinguish morphologies and, in certain cases, extract absolute or relative timing information. Finally, in suitable mixtures, the emission can be shifted from the VUV (\textit{Vacuum Ultraviolet}) to the near UV, visible or even NIR, spectral regions in which photodetection is technologically simpler and often less expensive \cite{bondara2010direct,morozov2012secondary,oliveira2012simulation,santorelli2021spectroscopic,bondar2012study}.

Naturally, scintillation readout also introduces limitations. The total optical efficiency depends not only on the gas, but also on geometry, solid angle, reflectivity, optical windows and the quantum efficiency or PDE of the photosensor. Unlike charge, light can undergo significant geometric losses and requires a more complete calibration of the detection system. Furthermore, photon production competes with non-radiative channels, collisional \textit{quenching} and, in some gases, attachment or dissociation processes that may degrade linearity or stability. At high gain, local saturation and space-charge effects can also appear in multiplication channels, which is particularly relevant when maximising optical output \cite{christophorou1999electron,amedo2023observation}. In other words, scintillation in MPGDs does not simply replace charge readout: it complements it and, in certain specific scenarios, can clearly outperform it.

Despite its instrumental interest, a quantitative prediction of gaseous scintillation remains a substantially less closed problem than charge transport. Relatively developed experimental programmes and models exist for certain specific situations. One important example arises from cosmic-ray physics, where collaborations such as AIRFLY and MACFLY measured the fluorescence yield of nitrogen and air, its spectral composition, and its dependence on pressure, temperature and humidity, with the aim of calibrating fluorescence telescopes used to reconstruct extensive atmospheric showers \cite{colin2007macfly,ave2008airfly}. Their parameterisation is, however, constructed for a highly specific problem: fluorescence of N$_2$ and air under atmospheric conditions, usually in the UV range, excited by relativistic electrons or shower particles. It is therefore not a general model capable of directly predicting the primary and secondary scintillation of arbitrary mixtures inside gaseous microstructures subjected to intense electric fields.

This shortcoming is especially relevant for MPGDs. In a real optical detector, knowing an isolated excitation cross section or a particular emission band is not sufficient. The final signal depends on a chain of coupled processes: microscopic production of excited and ionised states, energy transfer between species, recombination, molecular dissociation, \textit{quenching}, formation of excimers or excited fragments, radiative emission, absorption and possible optical feedback. Moreover, in mixtures containing noble gases and polyatomic molecules, the same deposited energy can be redistributed among very different channels: ionisation, atomic excitation, VUV emission, NIR lines, visible molecular bands or non-radiative processes. The lack of predictive models for arbitrary mixtures therefore directly limits the optimisation of optical detectors: it prevents an a priori estimate of how many photons will be produced, in which spectral range they will appear, how they will vary with pressure and concentration, and what compromises will arise between gain, stability, \textit{attachment}, useful emission and discharge damage.

The problem is not only practical, but also physical. A sufficiently complete scintillation model would make it possible to separate the electron-transport component, governed by electron--gas cross sections and the reduced electric field, from the subsequent kinetics of excited species. This separation is essential when extrapolating from one mixture to another, interpreting differences between primary and secondary emission, or studying effects that directly affect measurement, such as \textit{photon feedback}, photoionisation of impurities or surfaces, the appearance of metastable states, and the competition between radiative emission and collisional dissipation. From this perspective, constructing a phenomenological model and validating it against experimental data is not merely a fitting exercise, but a necessary step towards turning optical MPGD readout into a predictive and optimisable technique.

\subsection{Why Ar--CF$_4$, He--CF$_4$ and Ar--N$_2$?}

The viability of this strategy is no longer merely conceptual. Avalanche scintillation in gaseous microstructures has been demonstrated conclusively and, more importantly, experimental programmes now employ it as a detection principle. Secondary emission in GEM and MSGC structures operated with CF$_4$ mixtures was clearly observed years ago, showing that avalanche light can be measured with yields useful for instrumentation \cite{fraga2003the,morozov2012secondary}. In THGEM-based detectors, avalanche scintillation in argon has been directly observed using highly sensitive photosensors \cite{bondara2010direct}. More recently, microscopic simulation of electroluminescence in microstructures and the development of concepts such as FAT-GEMs have reinforced the idea that the boundary between gaseous multipliers and electroluminescent structures is increasingly blurred \cite{oliveira2012simulation,williams2024fatgems}. In parallel, experiments such as CYGNO have placed the optical readout of GEMs at the core of their detection strategy in gaseous TPCs for rare-process searches \cite{torelli2022the}. Along the same lines, DUNE is studying \emph{optical-based charge readout} technologies based on secondary light produced in amplification structures within gaseous argon, while in electroluminescent xenon detectors such as NEXT optical information is a central component of the instrumental design \cite{hayrapetyan2024dune,next2014present}.

In this context, Ar--CF$_4$ is a particularly attractive mixture. Argon provides a highly mature instrumental basis: it is abundant, inexpensive, well known in terms of electron transport and has extensively studied excitation and ionisation physics \cite{zatsarinny2013comparisons,biagi1999monte}. CF$_4$, in turn, is not merely a transport additive. It is also an actively scintillating gas, with ultraviolet and visible emissions associated with both CF$_4^{+*}$ and CF$_3^*$, making it an excellent candidate for optical readout \cite{washida1983emission,morozov2010photon,lehaut2015scintillation}. This combination also has an especially valuable property: energy transfer from excited argon species to emitting CF$_4$ states, giving rise to a marked \emph{wavelength-shifting} effect. In simple terms, part of the energy initially deposited in channels that are difficult to access with conventional photodetection can reappear as emission in bands better suited to standard optical sensors \cite{amedo2023observation,gonzlezdaz2024primary,veenhof2025primary}.

This possibility is highly relevant for MPGDs. One of the customary difficulties in noble-gas detectors is that a significant fraction of the emission lies in the VUV, where optical instrumentation is more demanding and light propagation is complicated by absorption in materials and surfaces. If a small concentration of CF$_4$ can convert part of that excitation into visible or near-UV light without compromising multiplier operation, the mixture immediately gains an instrumental advantage: the detector can continue to exploit the favourable microphysics of argon while gaining an optical-readout window that is much easier to handle. Recent observations indeed show that an intense visible component may appear in Ar--CF$_4$ even at low CF$_4$ concentrations, precisely as a consequence of these transfer and spectral-conversion mechanisms \cite{amedo2023observation,amedo2023scintillation,veenhof2025primary}. Rather than being merely a compromise mixture, Ar--CF$_4$ can therefore be understood as a mixture functionally designed to extract useful light from a gaseous microstructure.

Alongside Ar--CF$_4$, He--CF$_4$ plays a distinct but very important role in this work. Unlike argon, helium does not introduce, in the spectral range considered, an emission system comparable to the argon third continuum, Ar NIR lines or efficient transfer channels towards CF$_4$. He--CF$_4$ is therefore not expected to be dominated by \emph{wavelength shifting}; instead, its observed optical signal should arise essentially from the direct production of emitting CF$_4$ species, mainly CF$_4^{+*}$ and CF$_3^*$. This distinction makes He--CF$_4$ a particularly useful control case: if the direct parameters extracted from the Ar--CF$_4$ fit also describe He--CF$_4$ data, then the model is not merely reproducing a peculiarity of Ar--CF$_4$ transfer, but is capturing the intrinsic physics of CF$_4$ as a scintillating gas in a reasonable way.

From the validation point of view, this comparison is particularly powerful because changing the base gas completely modifies the electron microphysics. The cross sections of He and Ar differ, their excitation and ionisation thresholds are not the same, and the electron-energy distribution during the avalanche changes; the microscopic populations produced by Degrad or Garfield++ therefore change as well. Consequently, He--CF$_4$ makes it possible to test the model in an environment in which initial populations arise from different transport physics, while the radiative and kinetic parameters associated with the emitting CF$_4$ species should remain the same. In other words, He--CF$_4$ acts as an external check of the separation proposed in this work between microscopic production of excited states, determined by electron--gas cross sections, and the subsequent kinetic evolution of the emitting species.

Moreover, He--CF$_4$ is not a marginal mixture from an experimental point of view. Its scintillation has already been studied in GEM structures and in low-pressure gaseous detectors, in both primary and secondary emission, precisely because of the interest of CF$_4$ as a fast, luminous gas compatible with optical readout \cite{fraga2003the,FlorianPrimarySecundary}. The recent work by Brunbauer et al. on CF$_4$-based mixtures provides a particularly useful data set for this thesis, since it allows model predictions to be compared with independent measurements in a mixture in which argon transfer mechanisms are absent \cite{FlorianPrimarySecundary}. He--CF$_4$ is therefore introduced here not as a third mixture equivalent to Ar--CF$_4$ or Ar--N$_2$, but as a physical-consistency test: a different system, with different cross sections, governed by the same CF$_4$ emitting species.

Nevertheless, this choice also involves physical and technological costs. CF$_4$ introduces attachment channels and a more complex electron chemistry than a pure noble gas, so that effective gain, avalanche statistics and stability can depend very sensitively on reduced field and concentration \cite{christophorou1999electron,tsiledakis2004drift}. In addition, its environmental impact is a major disadvantage when considering large-scale detectors or unfavourable gas-circulation regimes. It is precisely for this reason that it is meaningful to investigate alternative mixtures capable of retaining part of the energy-transfer rationale without inheriting all the disadvantages of CF$_4$.

In this comparison, Ar--N$_2$ emerges as the most natural alternative. N$_2$ is inexpensive, chemically inert under operating conditions and environmentally much more benign than CF$_4$, making it attractive as an \emph{eco-friendly} option. Furthermore, even small amounts of N$_2$ can shift part of the argon emission towards the nitrogen second-positive band, in a spectral region much more accessible to conventional photodetection \cite{puech1977energy,morii2004quenching,friedl2012spectral}.

Consequently, the interest of this work is not limited to characterising specific gas mixtures, but lies in exploring a more general instrumental idea: the extent to which an MPGD can become a controlled scintillation device, in which the mixture is chosen not only for its transport or gain properties, but also for its ability to redistribute deposited energy into useful spectral bands. From this viewpoint, Ar--CF$_4$ represents a highly promising option for maximising optical signal and studying complex transfer mechanisms; He--CF$_4$ provides an independent test of the direct CF$_4$ component in a different base gas; and Ar--N$_2$ offers an excellent test bed for assessing how much of that functionality can be preserved with a far more sustainable additive. This comparison naturally frames the aim of this Master's thesis: to understand, model and predict the primary and secondary scintillation of gas mixtures relevant to optical detectors based on MPGDs.

\section{Objectives}

In this context, our work will focus on the study of primary and secondary scintillation in $\Ar-\CF_4$, $\He-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ mixtures under operating conditions relevant to optical gaseous detectors. The importance and novelty of this work lie in its ability to provide sound predictions of the absolute scintillation yield, both in primary and secondary emission, expressed as photons per deposited MeV or photons per electron generated in an avalanche. The aim is not merely to reproduce particular experimental data, but to construct a model capable of separating two distinct physical ingredients: on the one hand, the microscopic production of excited and ionised species, determined by electron--gas cross sections and obtained with Degrad or Garfield++; on the other hand, the subsequent kinetic evolution of those species, responsible for radiative emission, quenching, relaxation and possible energy transfers.

The $\Ar-\CF_4$ mixture will be the main system for studying transfer and \emph{wavelength-shifting} mechanisms. The effective parameters associated with direct CF$_4$ emission, the visible CF$_3^*$ contribution, the ultraviolet CF$_4^{+*}$ emission and the competition between emission, quenching and transfer from excited argon species will be fitted in this mixture. The $\He-\CF_4$ mixture will subsequently be used as an independent check of the model: since it does not exhibit the characteristic argon-transfer channels in the spectral range of interest, it allows the direct parameters obtained in $\Ar-\CF_4$ to be applied to a different base gas with distinct cross sections and microscopic populations. If the model provides a reasonable description of He--CF$_4$ data, this will constitute strong validation that the CF$_4$ parameters are not simply an accidental fit to the Ar--CF$_4$ mixture, but a transferable description of the emitting species of tetrafluoromethane. Finally, $\Ar-\N_2$ will allow an alternative spectral-conversion mechanism to be studied, based on energy transfer from excited argon states to the nitrogen second-positive band, with the additional advantage of being a far more environmentally favourable mixture.

This approach will make it possible not only to identify optimal operating points for optical gaseous detectors, but also to provide useful inputs for high-precision simulations with codes such as Geant4 and Garfield++. Its applications include the design of amplification structures, estimation of optical signals in different geometries, optimisation of mixtures, gas-purity studies and the construction of predictive models for detectors based on optical readout. Accordingly, the specific objectives are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item To characterise the experimental scintillation of the $\Ar-\CF_4$, $\He-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ gas mixtures, introducing the intrinsic emissions of each gas, the relevant radiative channels and the transfer or absence-of-transfer phenomena that dominate in each case.

    \item To construct kinetic models for the ultraviolet (200--400 nm), visible (400--680 nm) and near-infrared (NIR, 680--800 nm) emission of the $\Ar-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ mixtures, incorporating direct production, energy transfer, relaxation, dissociation, radiative emission and collisional quenching.

    \item To determine and optimise the effective model parameters from global fits to primary and secondary scintillation, separating, insofar as possible, the parameters associated with the direct production of CF$_4$ emitting species from those specifically linked to transfer from excited argon states.

    \item To use the $\He-\CF_4$ mixture as a consistency test of the CF$_4$ kinetic model: to apply the direct parameters obtained in $\Ar-\CF_4$ to a different base gas with different cross sections, and to compare the predictions with experimental primary- and secondary-scintillation data available in the literature \cite{fraga2003the,FlorianPrimarySecundary}.

    \item To produce predictions of absolute primary scintillation induced by X-rays or ionising particles for the $\Ar-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ mixtures over a broad range of pressures (1--5 bar) and concentrations (0--100\%), including spectral predictions between 200 and 800 nm and comparison with experimental results.

    \item To produce primary- and secondary-scintillation predictions for argon infrared emission between 680 and 800 nm in the $\Ar-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ mixtures, over the pressure range studied (1--5 bar). For the secondary emission of $\Ar-\CF_4$, comparisons will be made with absolute experimental secondary yields in the range 0.05--1 bar.

    \item To produce secondary-scintillation predictions over a broad range of pressures (0.05--10 bar), concentrations (0--100\%), electric fields and characteristic amplification sizes. In particular, the dependence of optical yield on electron gain, reduced field and effective MPGD gap will be studied using microscopic simulations in C++ and Garfield++.

    \item To critically compare the behaviour of $\Ar-\CF_4$, $\He-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ as scintillation systems for optical readout: $\Ar-\CF_4$ as a high-optical-yield mixture with strong transfer, $\He-\CF_4$ as a test of direct CF$_4$ emission, and $\Ar-\N_2$ as a more sustainable alternative based on transfer towards nitrogen.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Spectra} \label{sec:experimental_espectra}

\subsection{Ar} \label{subsec:Ar_spectra}

The scintillation of noble gases (Ar, Kr, Xe, He and Ne) was among the first to be studied because of their ability to produce spectral bands in the VUV, namely at 126 nm for Ar, 146 nm for Kr and 178 nm for Xe. In these early studies, the term ``second continuum'' was introduced to describe the observed scintillation, since it was not associated with an atomic transition. In fact, these emissions are associated with transitions between excimers of the different species. An excimer is a dimer in which one of the atoms is excited; in the case of noble gases, excimers are stable, unlike ground-state dimers.

\begin{minipage}{0.6\linewidth}

However, when the wavelength domain was extended, phenomena that could not be directly associated with the second continuum were observed. This emission was termed the \textbf{third continuum}, denoted as $X_{\third}$. It is now known that the third continuum may arise from different species, such as $X_2^{+,*}$ or $X_2^{++,(*)}$. Depending on the gas and pressure, this feature has a FWHM ranging from 10 to 100 nm over the interval from 60 to 400 nm. In argon, its peak can shift from approximately 230 nm at 0.5 bar to approximately 270 nm at 2 bar. \subsection{CF$_4$}

Within the wavelength range of interest, CF$_4$ scintillates through the decay of two species, $\CF_4^{+,*}$ and $\CF_3^*$. The visible region, with a peak near 630 nm, is associated with the transition

\begin{equation*}
\CF_3^*(2A_2'',1E') \rightarrow \CF_3(1A_1') + h \nu \;(\SI{630}{nm})
\end{equation*}

\cite{washida1983emission}. In the ultraviolet, several species may scintillate. The dominant primary-emission bands are associated with

\begin{equation*}
\CF_4^{+,*} (\tilde{C}) \rightarrow  \CF_4^{+,*} (\tilde{A},\tilde{X})+ h \nu \;(\SI{290}{nm},\SI{230}{nm}),
\end{equation*}

with emission at 230 and 290 nm. The UV emission from $\CF_3^*$, which is important for secondary scintillation, is described by

\begin{equation*}
\CF_3^*(1A_1') \rightarrow \CF_3(1A_1') + h \nu \;(\SI{260}{nm}).
\end{equation*}

The transition

\begin{equation*}
\CF_4^{+,*} (\tilde{D}) \rightarrow  \CF_4^{+,*} (\tilde{A})+ h \nu \;(\SI{364}{nm})
\end{equation*}

is also present in pure CF$_4$ emission.

The emissions of pure \CFt\ are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:experimentalCF4GlobalEspectra}.

Most scintillating species are produced by direct electron collisions, whether the electrons are primary or secondary, although some precursors may ultimately generate these species. In pure gases, the number of precursors feeding a particular emission is very limited. When an additional component is introduced, new interaction channels open and new precursors of the scintillating species appear.

\section{Kinetic Model} \label{Sec:model_kin}

\subsection{Introduction to Kinetic Models}

Kinetic models for the description of gases are analogous to the collisional--radiative models used in plasma physics. Their aim is to describe the evolution of populations of atoms, molecules, ions and excited states when production, energy-transfer, collisional-relaxation and radiative-emission processes act simultaneously. In many scintillation problems it is not necessary to follow the complete particle distribution function; instead, the dynamics of the populations of the relevant species can be described by balance equations.

For a species or excited state $i$, the general kinetic equation can be written as
\begin{equation}
    \dv{N_i}{t}
    =
    S_i
    +
    \sum_j N_j R_{j\to i}
    -
    N_i \sum_k R_{i\to k},
    \label{eq:general_kinetic_balance}
\end{equation}
where $N_i$ is the population of species $i$, $S_i$ represents its direct production, and the coefficients $R_{j\to i}$ and $R_{i\to k}$ group the formation and destruction rates due to collisions, charge transfers, dissociations, non-radiative relaxation or radiative decay.

For a particular excited state, destruction is generally governed by the competition between spontaneous emission and collisional de-excitation. If state $i$ has a radiative lifetime $\tau_i$ and can be deactivated through collisions with different species $m$, of density $n_m$, with kinetic coefficients $K_{iQ(m)}$, its total disappearance rate is
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma_i^{\text{tot}}
    =
    \frac{1}{\tau_i}
    +
    \sum_m n_m K_{iQ(m)}.
    \label{eq:total_loss_rate}
\end{equation}

Under stationary conditions, $\dv*{N_i}{t}=0$, the population of the state is determined by the ratio of its total production rate to its total loss rate:
\begin{equation}
    N_i
    =
    \frac{S_i + \sum_j N_j R_{j\to i}}
    {\Gamma_i^{\text{tot}}}.
    \label{eq:stationary_population}
\end{equation}

In many cases, more useful than the absolute population is the probability that an excited state evolves through a specific channel. If a process $x$ competes with all other de-excitation channels, its probability is given by the ratio of its partial rate to the total rate:
\begin{equation}
    P_{i\to x}
    =
    \frac{\Gamma_{i\to x}}
    {\Gamma_i^{\text{tot}}}.
    \label{eq:branching_ratio_general}
\end{equation}
Thus, if channel $x$ corresponds to a collision with species $m$, one obtains
\begin{equation}
    P_{i\to m}
    =
    \frac{n_m K_{iQ(m)}}
    {\dfrac{1}{\tau_i} + \sum_\ell n_\ell K_{iQ(\ell)}},
    \label{eq:branching_collision}
\end{equation}
whereas, if the channel considered is radiative,
\begin{equation}
    P_{i\to \gamma}
    =
    \frac{1/\tau_i}
    {\dfrac{1}{\tau_i} + \sum_\ell n_\ell K_{iQ(\ell)}}.
    \label{eq:branching_radiative}
\end{equation}

In a generic binary mixture $A-B$, with total density $n$ and molar fraction $f$ of $A$, the partial densities are
\begin{equation}
    n_{A}=n\,f,
    \qquad
    n_{B}=n\,(1-f).
\end{equation}
The evolution probabilities of an excited state are therefore fixed by the competition between collisional channels with argon, collisional channels with tetrafluoromethane or nitrogen, and intrinsic radiative decays. This structure directly yields expressions of the form
\begin{equation}
    P
    =
    \frac{n\,f\,K_{\text{channel}}}
    {n\,f\,K_{\text{channel}}
    +
    n\,(1-f)\,K_{\text{competition}}
    +
    1/\tau},
    \label{eq:generic_probability_binary_mixture}
\end{equation}
which are precisely those used in the kinetic model describing scintillation in the $\Ar-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ mixtures. The Loschmidt constant
\begin{equation*}
 n_0 = \SI{2.6868e25}{m^{-3}}
\end{equation*}
gives the number of molecules in an ideal gas at 1 atm and 273.15 K. Hence, when only the pressure is changed, the molecular density can be written as
\begin{equation*}
 n = (p/p_0) \cdot n_0.
\end{equation*}
In this work, $n_0$ is included within the kinetic coefficients $K$, which then have units of ns$^{-1}$. From this point onwards, $n=p/p_0$, with $p_0=\SI{1}{bar}$.

Using these branching probabilities, the emission yield associated with a given spectral band can be constructed as the sum of all production routes feeding the scintillating species, weighted by the probability that the species forms and subsequently emits radiatively before being collisionally quenched.

\subsection{Ar--CF$_4$} \label{subbsec:kinArCF4}

\subsubsection{Interactions}

For the emission of $\CF_4(\tilde{C})^{+,*}$ and of the third continuum, the relevant interaction mechanisms are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Transfer and quenching of the third continuum with $\CF_4$:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar_{n=2,3}^{+,*},
        \Ar_{n=2,3}^{++,(*)} + \CF_4
        \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar_\third Q(\CF_4)}}  2(3) \Ar^{(+)} + \CF_4^{(+,*)}.
    \end{equation}
    \item Third-continuum emission:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar_{n=2,3}^{+,*},
        \Ar_{n=2,3}^{++,(*)} + \CF_4
        \xrightarrow{\tau_\third} 2(3) \Ar + h \nu \;(80--\SI{300}{nm}).
    \end{equation}
    \item Relaxation of $\CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v)$ to $\CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v=0)$:
    \begin{equation}
         \CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v) \xrightarrow{K_{\mathrm{relax}}}  \CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v=0).
    \end{equation}
    \item Dissociation of $\CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v)$:
    \begin{equation}
         \CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v) \xrightarrow{\tau_{\mathrm{diss}}} \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}
    \item Emission of $\CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v=0)$:
    \begin{equation}
         \CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v=0) \xrightarrow{\tau_{\mathrm{uv}}}
         \CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{A},\tilde{X}) + h \nu \;(290,230\,\mathrm{nm}).
    \end{equation}
    \item Quenching of $\CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v=0)$ with another $\CF_4$ molecule:
    \begin{equation}
         \CF_4^{+,*}(\tilde{C},v) + \CF_4 \xrightarrow{K_{\CF_4 Q(\CF_4)}} \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

For the emission of $\CF_3^*(2A_2',1E_1')$, the following processes must be considered:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Quenching of excited argon states $\Ar^{**}$ with argon:
    \begin{equation}
         \Ar^{**} + \Ar \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar^{**},Q(\Ar)}}  \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}
    \item Quenching of $\Ar^{**}$ with $\CF_4$:
    \begin{equation}
         \Ar^{**} + \CF_4 \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar^{**},Q(\CF_4)}}  \Ar^{(*)} + \CF_3^{(*)} + \mathrm{F}^{(*)}.
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Ar--CF$_4$ Kinetic Model}

Once the number of precursors ($\Ar^{**}$, $\Ar^{+,*}_{n=2,3}$, $\Ar^{++,(*)}_{n=2,3}$, $\CF_3^*$ and $\CF_4^{+,*}$) produced in an X-ray or electron interaction is known, the kinetic parameters introduced above can be combined with effective probabilities or fractions to obtain the number of photons. Effective probabilities or fractions are necessary because, in some cases, the number of states belonging to each particular species, such as $\CF_3^*(\tilde{C})$, is not directly available; rather, only the number of general states, such as the total number of $\CF_3^*$ excitations, is known. These effective probabilities provide a theoretical mechanism that preserves the simplicity of the model while allowing realistic assumptions about transfer processes to be made.

For $\CF_4^{+,*}$, $N_{\CF_3^+}$ is used instead of $N_{\CF_4^{+,(*)}}$, since information on the latter states is not available. This approximation is valid because all $\CF_4^+$ states eventually dissociate into $\CF_3^{+,(*)}$ \cite{zhang1989excitation}.

The scintillation of an $\Ar-\CF_4$ mixture therefore contains three emission components. The visible emission from $\CF_3^*$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    Y_{\CF_3,\vis}
    =
    N_{\CF_3}\cdot \left. P_{\CF_3^*}\right|_{\vis,\dir}
    +
    N_{\Ar^{**}} \cdot P_{\Ar^{**}}
    \cdot
    \frac{n \cdot f \cdot K_{\Ar^{**}Q(\CF_4)}}{
    n \cdot f \cdot K_{\Ar^{**}Q(\CF_4)}
    +
    n \cdot (1-f) \cdot K_{\Ar^{**}Q(\Ar)}
    +
    1/\tau_{\Ar^{**}}}. \label{eq:YvisCF3}
\end{equation}
The parameter $\left. P_{\CF_3^*}\right|_{\dir}$ is the probability that $\CF_3$ excitations ($N_{\CF_3}$) belong to a scintillating channel and eventually emit. The parameter $P_{\Ar^{**}}$ is the product of the fraction of excited states that are energetically capable of transferring to $\CF_3^*$ and the probability that this transfer produces an emitting $\CF_3$ species.

The ultraviolet emission from $\CF_4^{+,*}$ is modelled as
\begin{equation}
    Y_{\CF_4,\uv}
    =
    P_{\CF_4^{+,*}}
    \cdot
    \bqty{
      N_{\CF_3^+}\cdot \left. P_{\CF_4^{+,*}} \right|_{\dir}
      +
      N_{\Ar^{++}}\cdot P_{\Ar^{++}}
      \pqty{
        \frac{n \cdot f \cdot K_{\Ar^{++}Q(\CF_4)}}{
        1/\tau_{\third} + n \cdot f \cdot K_{\Ar^{++}Q(\CF_4)}}
      }
    }. \label{eq:YuvCF4}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    P_{\CF_4^{+,*}}
    =
    \frac{n \cdot f \cdot K_{\text{relax}}}{
    n \cdot f \cdot K_{\text{relax}} + 1/\tau_{\text{dissc}}}
    \cdot
    \frac{1/\tau_{\uv}}{
    1/\tau_{\uv} + n \cdot f \cdot K_{\CF_4^{+,*}Q(\CF_4)}}.
\end{equation}
Here, $\left. P_{\CF_4^{+,*}} \right|_{\dir}$ is the fraction of associated states, while $P_{\Ar^{++}}$ is the probability that the interaction of a third-continuum precursor with a $\CF_4$ molecule generates $\CF_4(\tilde{C},v)$. Furthermore, $\CF_3^*$ may decay at 260 nm. Since this process follows the same mechanisms as the visible emission and its observed peak is substantially smaller than the visible peaks, it can be directly parameterised as the product of an effective probability and the visible emission:
\begin{equation}
    Y_{\CF_3,\uv}
    = \left. P_{\CF_3^*}\right|_{\uv,\dir} \cdot
    Y_{\CF_3,\vis}.     \label{eq:YuvCF3}
\end{equation}

Finally, the ultraviolet emission associated with the $\Ar_{\third}$ component is
\begin{equation}
    Y_{\Ar_{\third},\uv}
    =
    N_{\Ar^{++}}
    \cdot
    \pqty{
      \frac{1/\tau_{\third}}{
      1/\tau_{\third} + n \cdot f \cdot K_{\Ar^{++}Q(\CF_4)}}
    }. \label{eq:YuvAr3rd}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Ar--N$_2$} \label{subbsec:kinArN2}

\subsubsection{Kinetic Processes}

Higher argon excited states, denoted by $\Ar^{**}$, always decay to one of the resonant (1s5) and metastable (1s4) excitations through the following processes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Decay of $\Ar^{**}$ to $\Ar_{1s4,1s5}$:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar^{**}
        \xrightarrow{\tau_{\Ar^{**}}}  \Ar_{1s4,1s5} + h \nu \;(\text{IR}).
    \end{equation}
    \item Quenching with nitrogen:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar^{**} + \N_2
        \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar^{**}Q(\N_2)}}  \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}
    \item Quenching with argon:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar^{**} + \Ar
        \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar^{**}Q(\Ar)}}  \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

The resonant (1s5) and metastable (1s4) argon states may then undergo the following interactions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Quenching of $\Ar_{1s4,1s5}$ with two argon atoms:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar_{1s4,1s5} + 2 \Ar
        \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar_{1s4,1s5,Q(2\Ar)}}}  \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}

    \item Transfer of $\Ar_{1s4,1s5}$ with $\N_2$ resulting in non-radiative $\N_2$:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar_{1s4,1s5} + \N_2
        \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar_{1s4,1s5,Q(2\Ar)}}}  \Ar + \N_2^{(*)}.
    \end{equation}

    \item Transfer of $\Ar_{1s4,1s5}$ with $\N_2$ resulting in $\N_2(C)$:
    \begin{equation}
        \Ar_{1s4,1s5} + \N_2
        \xrightarrow{K_{\Ar_{1s4,1s5,Q(\N_2(C))}}}  \Ar + \N_2^*(C).
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

The relevant processes involving $\N_2$ are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Quenching of $\N_2(C)$ with $\Ar$:
    \begin{equation}
        \N_2(C) +  \Ar
        \xrightarrow{K_{\N_2,Q(\Ar)}}  \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}

    \item Quenching of $\N_2(C)$ with $\N_2$:
    \begin{equation}
        \N_2(C) +  \N_2
        \xrightarrow{K_{\N_2,Q(\N_2)}}  \text{Non-radiative states}.
    \end{equation}

    \item Decay of $\N_2(C)$:
    \begin{equation}
        \N_2(C,v=0) \xrightarrow{\tau_{\N_2(C)}}  \N_2(B) + h\nu \;(330--410\,\mathrm{nm}).
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

They can, however, be populated by the decay of more energetic excitations. Consequently, $N_{\Ar^*,\lambda}$ is the sum of all excitations more energetic than the emitting state, and $P_{\Ar^*,\lambda}$ is the product of the fraction of states genuinely involved and the probability that they reach the scintillating species. Once that species is reached, radiative decay competes with quenching. The kinetic model for infrared emission at wavelength $\lambda$ is therefore

\begin{equation}
    Y_{\mathrm{IR},\lambda} = P_{\Ar^*,\lambda} \cdot N_{\Ar^*,\lambda}  \cdot \pqty{\frac{1 / \tau_{\Ar^*,\lambda}}{1 / \tau_{\Ar^*,\lambda} + n \cdot f \cdot  K_{\Ar^*, Q(\Ar), \lambda} + n \cdot(1-f) \cdot  K_{\Ar^*, Q(\CF_4/\N_2), \lambda}} }.
\end{equation}

\section{Simulations}

To obtain the mean population of each state per event, $N_X$, two complementary tools were used: Degrad for the description of the primary interaction and Garfield++ for the description of secondary scintillation. This combination naturally separates two distinct physical scales. On the one hand, creation of the primary signal depends on the incident particle type and its initial ionisation mechanisms. On the other hand, avalanche development, excitation production and the generation of secondary electrons are governed by the local electric field and by the electron--atom/molecule cross sections of the mixture.

From the kinetic point of view, the fundamental input quantities are the cross sections $\sigma_j(\varepsilon)$ associated with each microscopic channel $j$ (elastic collision, excitation, ionisation, attachment, \emph{etc.}), where $\varepsilon$ is the kinetic energy of the electron. In practical terms, this means that the populations $N_X$ per event can be interpreted as the accumulated result of all excitation, ionisation or attachment collisions undergone by the primary electron and all of its descendants throughout the avalanche.

Since the same set of cross sections is available in both cases, populations obtained with Degrad and Garfield++ can be regarded as equivalent model inputs even though they correspond to different phenomena. Thus, a model capable of using Degrad information to describe primary scintillation may also be capable of describing secondary scintillation using Garfield++. The cross sections employed for $\Ar$, $\CF_4$ and $\N_2$, shown in Figures~\ref{fig:ArCs}, \ref{fig:CF4Cs} and \ref{fig:N2Cs}, distinguish between elastic, ionisation, attachment and inelastic/excitation channels.

\subsection{Garfield++}

Garfield++ is a \texttt{C++} environment intended for the detailed simulation of gaseous and semiconductor detectors based on ionisation. In the context of gases, its main interest lies in its ability to track electrons microscopically, collision by collision, using the interaction rates tabulated by Magboltz. Since Magboltz, also developed by Steve Biagi, calculates transport properties and collision rates from microscopic cross sections, the combination of Garfield++ and Magboltz connects scattering microphysics to macroscopic observables such as drift, diffusion, the Townsend coefficient, attachment, gain and the production of excited states.

In this work, Garfield++ is employed in microscopic electron-tracking mode. Within this scheme, the electron propagates between collisions along a classical trajectory determined by the local electric field. The free-flight duration is sampled from the total collision frequency, and the type of interaction occurring at the end of the step is selected according to the relative rates of each process. This procedure is especially appropriate for mixtures with strongly competing channels, such as $\Ar$, $\CF_4$ or mixtures with small additions of $\N_2$, in which excitation, ionisation, attachment and inelastic losses coexist with different energy thresholds.

The algorithm used by Garfield++ to sample the flight time is the null-collision Monte Carlo method, also known as MCC or the NC technique. Its basic idea is to introduce an additional fictitious frequency that allows free flights to be sampled using a simple majorant rate; whenever the sampled process corresponds to a ``null collision'', the particle does not change its physical state and simply continues moving. Although these virtual collisions have no direct physical meaning, the method correctly reproduces the statistics of real processes and is highly efficient when collision rates depend strongly on electron energy. In Garfield++, virtual collisions explicitly appear in the internal process classification as \textit{virtual (null) collisions}.

\subsection{Uniform-Field Approximation and Avalanche Development}

For the simulation of secondary multiplication, a uniform-drift geometry is adopted. This approximation assumes that, in the effective region where the avalanche develops, the electric field is approximately constant:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}) \simeq E_0\, \hat{\mathbf{z}}.
\end{equation}
The hypothesis is reasonable when the amplification gap is small and the main objective is to estimate collision statistics, gain and the mean production of excitations, rather than to resolve the local non-uniformities of the real geometry in maximum detail. Under this approximation, differences between electrons arise mainly from the random sequence of collisions rather than from geometrical field variations.

The electron avalanche is then built hierarchically. The primary electron, introduced directly in Garfield++, crosses the drift region and, whenever its energy between collisions exceeds the relevant thresholds, may produce excitations or ionisations. Each ionisation collision generates a new secondary electron, which is in turn transported microscopically by the same algorithm and may produce additional ionisations. The final result is a branched cascade, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:uniformDrift}, in which the primary electron and all its descendants contribute to the total gain and to the populations $N_X$ of the different excited species. Figure~\ref{fig:gainNe} shows the simulated electron-gain distribution. The number of electrons produced per simulated primary electron is called the electron, or charge, gain and is denoted by $n_e$.

Equivalently, on average, $N_X$ is determined by integrating the collision probabilities along all primary- and secondary-electron trajectories. This is precisely the magnitude subsequently introduced into the kinetic--radiative model in order to estimate the various emission channels of the mixture.

\section{Parameter Determination}

As anticipated above, many parameters of the Ar--CF$_4$ model are unknown, including quenching rates and lifetimes, while others are introduced as effective probabilities or fractions in order to relate the populations simulated in Degrad or Garfield++ to real scintillation. In the case of Ar--N$_2$, most parameters reported in the literature differ substantially from one another, and effective probabilities are also required. For nitrogen, the kinetic parameters are therefore semi-fixed: they are allowed to vary within an assigned interval around the mean value obtained from experimental information.

In order to determine these parameters, experimental data must be fitted together with simulated populations evaluated at the same concentrations as the experimental points. It should be stressed that the $\Ar-\CF_4$ and $\Ar-\N_2$ fits are independent. The following considerations are taken into account in the fitting procedure:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The experimental data used in the primary fits have a common arbitrary normalisation, particularly for Ar--CF$_4$, Ar--N$_2$ and the infrared measurements, using the standard Ar--CF$_4$ 95--5 candle for both visible and ultraviolet data. To account for this, a normalisation factor $N_{\norma}$ is included in front of all yield equations.

    \item The experimental measurements provide the number of photons per electron generated in the primary event, whereas Degrad provides the number of populations per photon. Rescaling therefore requires the mean ionisation potential $W(f)$, which depends on concentration and mixture. This factor multiplies the experimental data in order to represent them in ph/eV. For Ar--CF$_4$, experimental values from Ref.~\cite{reinking1986studies} are used, interpolating at intermediate concentrations. For Ar--N$_2$, the values of Ref.~\cite{reinking1986studies} are used at 100\% concentration, while intermediate points are described by the composition function
    \begin{equation}
        W_I(f_{\N_2}) = \frac{1}{f_{\N_2}/W_{\N_2} + (1-f_{\N_2})/W_{\Ar}}.
    \end{equation}

    The corresponding mean-ionisation-potential curves are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ionization_potential}.

    \item For CF$_4$, the available experimental data cover two wavelength ranges: the visible region (400--700 nm) and the ultraviolet region (200--400 nm). \subsection{Treatment of Statistical and Systematic Uncertainties}

The experimental data employed in the fit generally present two main contributions to their uncertainty: a statistical component and a systematic component. These components have different physical and experimental origins and should therefore not be treated in the same manner. Statistical uncertainties describe independent random fluctuations associated, for example, with the finite number of events, measurement dispersion or the procedure used to extract experimental points. Systematic uncertainties, by contrast, are usually associated with common effects such as calibration, optical efficiency, normalisation, spectral response or the absolute intensity scale. Hence, while the statistical component can, to a first approximation, be regarded as independent from point to point, the systematic component must be treated as a correlated displacement affecting several points in the same data set. This distinction is standard in statistical analyses of particle-physics experiments, where systematic uncertainties are introduced through Gaussian-constrained auxiliary or \textit{nuisance parameters} \cite{Barlow2002,Cowan2011,Conway2011,Cranmer2012HistFactory,PDGStatistics2024}.

In this work, the nominal fit is performed using the statistical uncertainties of the experimental data. Given a model $f(x;\boldsymbol{\theta})$, where $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ represents the vector of free parameters, the optimal parameters $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ are obtained by minimising the sum of weighted residuals,

\begin{equation*}
r_i(\boldsymbol{\theta})=
\frac{y_i-f(x_i;\boldsymbol{\theta})}
{\sigma_{i,\mathrm{stat}}},
\end{equation*}

using the \texttt{least\_squares} routine from \texttt{scipy.optimize} \cite{Virtanen2020SciPy}. This choice defines the central model curve, interpreted as the best description of the data under the physical hypotheses incorporated in the model. The exclusive use of statistical uncertainty in the objective function prevents systematic components, which do not represent independent fluctuations, from being treated as point-by-point random noise. In particular, directly adding systematic uncertainties in quadrature within each residual would yield a fit less sensitive to the structure of the data and would ignore correlations between points induced by a common systematic source.

A pseudoexperiment-based procedure is used to propagate uncertainties to model parameters and predictions. This method is especially appropriate here because the model is non-linear and significant degeneracies exist among some parameters. Under these conditions, the local estimate of the covariance matrix based on the fit Jacobian,

\begin{equation*}
\mathrm{Cov}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})
\simeq
s^2(J^TJ)^{-1},
\end{equation*}

may be numerically unstable or difficult to interpret. In particular, individual parameters may have very large uncertainties owing to internal correlations in the model, even when the final physical prediction remains relatively well determined. For this reason, uncertainties are propagated directly by means of fluctuated replicas of the data, refitting the model for each replica. In practice, this procedure is equivalent to a Monte Carlo propagation of the experimental uncertainties and avoids relying solely on a linear approximation around the minimum.

For the statistical component, pseudoexperiments are generated by fluctuating each point independently,

\begin{equation*}
y_i^{(t)}
=
y_i
+
\epsilon_i^{(t)},
\qquad
\epsilon_i^{(t)}
\sim
\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{i,\mathrm{stat}}),
\end{equation*}

where $t$ identifies the generated replica. Each fluctuated data set is fitted again with the same model, yielding a parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)}$ and an associated prediction $f(x;\boldsymbol{\theta}^{(t)})$. The distribution of these predictions directly estimates the statistical uncertainty of the fitted curve. In the resulting representations, the statistical band is defined by the 16th and 84th percentiles of the obtained curves, corresponding approximately to a one-standard-deviation band for Gaussian distributions.

The systematic component is treated differently, since it does not correspond to independent fluctuations of each point. Common Gaussian variables, or \textit{nuisance parameters}, are introduced for each relevant systematic source. If $\Delta_i^{(k)}$ represents the systematic displacement of point $i$ due to source $k$, a systematic pseudoexperiment is constructed as

\begin{equation*}
y_i^{(t)}
=
y_i
+
\sum_k z_k^{(t)}\Delta_i^{(k)},
\qquad
z_k^{(t)}
\sim
\mathcal{N}(0,1).
\end{equation*}

In this way, all points affected by a common systematic source move coherently. For instance, separate systematic sources can be considered for the UV and visible Ar--CF$_4$ data, while in the infrared it is natural to assign one systematic source to each wavelength. In Ar--N$_2$, where the data are arranged into fewer channels, one common systematic source may be assigned to the relevant set. This treatment more accurately reflects the physical meaning of a systematic uncertainty: an optical calibration or a spectral efficiency does not shift every point randomly, but modifies an entire family of measurements in a correlated manner.

Formally, this procedure is equivalent to constructing a total covariance matrix with a diagonal statistical part and a correlated systematic part,

\begin{equation*}
V
=
V_{\mathrm{stat}}
+
V_{\mathrm{syst}},
\end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation*}
V_{\mathrm{stat},ij}
=
\sigma_{i,\mathrm{stat}}^2\delta_{ij},
\end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*}
V_{\mathrm{syst},ij}
=\sum_k
\Delta_i^{(k)}\Delta_j^{(k)}.
\end{equation*}

This expression of the covariance matrix makes explicit that a common systematic source generates correlations between different experimental points. The pseudoexperiment approach avoids the need to explicitly construct and invert this matrix in every fit, while preserving the physical interpretation of systematic correlations. It is therefore a simple and transparent version of the auxiliary-parameter treatment conventionally employed in high-energy analyses, including \textit{profile-likelihood} methods and \textsc{HistFactory}-type implementations \cite{Cowan2011,Cranmer2012HistFactory}.

Finally, parameter uncertainties are obtained from the spread of the fitted values in the different pseudoexperiments. A statistical contribution, derived from replicas with independent point-by-point fluctuations, and a systematic contribution, derived from replicas with correlated shifts, are calculated separately for every parameter. Final model predictions for primary yields in $\mathrm{ph/MeV}$ are accompanied by bands constructed from percentiles of the refitted curves. This treatment provides a clear separation between statistical fit stability and sensitivity to common systematic sources, while retaining a physically consistent interpretation of both contributions. When some parameters exhibit large relative uncertainties, these should be interpreted with caution, since they may reflect internal degeneracies in the model rather than an equivalent loss of precision in the final prediction. Particular emphasis is therefore placed on the model prediction bands, which provide a more robust estimate of the uncertainty affecting physically observable quantities.

\begin{equation}
    P_{\Ar^{**}} = P_{\Ar^{**}}^* \cdot f_{\Ar^{**}} (\varepsilon_{\Ar^{**}}) \qquad f_{\Ar^{**}} (\varepsilon_{\Ar^{**}}) = \frac{\sum_{i} \left( \theta (\epsilon_i > \varepsilon_{\Ar^{**}}) \cdot  N_{\Ar^{**}} (\varepsilon_i) \right)}{\sum_{i} N_{\Ar^{**}} (\varepsilon_i)}.

\end{equation}

Here $\theta$ is the step function and $\varepsilon_i$ is the energy of each state. The values $\varepsilon_{\Ar^{**}}$ and $\varepsilon_{\CF_3^{*}}$ are the lower energy limits, or \textit{thresholds}, for $\Ar^{**}$ and $\CF_3^{*}$, respectively. The following notation will therefore be used:

\begin{equation}
    N(\varepsilon) = f (\varepsilon) \cdot N.
\end{equation}

The lower energy limit adopted in the parameter evaluation is 12.9 eV for both Ar$^{**}$ and CF$_3^{*}$. Excitations of the $\Ar(p)$ type begin above 12.9 eV. Sadeghi et al.\ \cite{Sadeghi} showed that transfers from $p$-type excited states of Ar$^{**}$ are one order of magnitude more effective than transfers from $s$-type excited states. The quality of the secondary predictions therefore provides a means of assessing the adequacy of these thresholds.

This procedure cannot be carried out for third-continuum states or for $\CF_4^{*,+}$, because information on these states is unavailable in both Degrad and Garfield++, as noted above. This limitation significantly hinders the reproduction of secondary results. Improved experimental cross sections for both gases would allow this identification procedure to be implemented in the future.

\begin{equation}
    Y(\lambda, f_X, p)  = \sum_i Y_i(f_X, p) \cdot \text{gauss}(\lambda, \mu = \lambda_i, \sigma = \sigma_i).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Secondary-Scintillation Predictions}

GEMs, FAT-GEMs and TH-GEMs are three of the MPGD structures most commonly used to produce controlled electron avalanches in gaseous detectors. Although these structures present markedly different geometries, from thicknesses of tens of micrometres in GEMs to several millimetres in FAT-GEMs, all share the same physical idea: concentrating intense electric fields in small regions in order to favour electron multiplication and, simultaneously, the production of excited states responsible for secondary scintillation.

These geometrical differences are important because they directly modify the effective avalanche length, the number of electron--gas collisions and the relationship between ionisation, excitation and non-radiative losses. In a thin structure such as a conventional GEM, the avalanche develops over a short distance and at very high fields. In a TH-GEM or FAT-GEM, by contrast, the amplification region is more extended, allowing an electron to accumulate a larger number of collisions before leaving the active region. Consequently, for comparable electron gain, thicker structures may produce more photons per avalanche electron, particularly when the reduced field $E/p$ lies in a region favourable to excitation. Typical dimensions and operating conditions are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:mgpd}.

This section studies predictions of secondary scintillation in $\Ar-\CF_4$ and $\He-\CF_4$ mixtures, expressed as photons per avalanche electron, ph/e$^-$. The strategy is the same as throughout the work: microscopic production of excited populations, obtained with Garfield++, is separated from the subsequent kinetic evolution of those species, described by the model developed in Section~\ref{Sec:model_kin}. The fundamental difference with respect to the primary case is that the populations now arise not from an initial X-ray or $\alpha$-particle interaction, but from the development of an electron avalanche in an intense-field region.

The main aim of these predictions is not merely to reproduce specific experimental points, but to test whether parameters obtained in the primary regime can be transferred to the avalanche regime. This is a demanding test: the electric field modifies the electron-energy distribution, the relative frequency of excitation and ionisation, and the importance of the energy thresholds of each channel. Consequently, good agreement in the secondary regime would indicate that the model not only fits primary data, but also reasonably captures the link between microscopic populations and optical emission.

\subsubsection{Comparison with Experimental Data}

Model predictions are compared with the available experimental secondary-scintillation data in visible, ultraviolet and near-infrared emission for Ar--CF$_4$, and in CF$_4$ emission for He--CF$_4$. The following convention is used in those comparisons: filled points correspond to indirect estimates of optical yield, obtained from intermediate experimental quantities or auxiliary conversions, whereas open points correspond to direct experimental estimates of the number of photons produced per avalanche electron. This distinction is important because indirect estimates may carry additional uncertainties associated with gain, optical efficiency, normalisation or the reconstruction procedure.

The prediction of visible emission in $\Ar-\CF_4$ is dominated by $\CF_3^*$ emission around 630 nm. This is one of the most relevant regions from an instrumental point of view, since it lies in a spectral window accessible to cameras, SiPMs and conventional photodetectors. In this case, the band does not represent a purely statistical uncertainty from the primary fit, but the variation among the different parameter-identification schemes discussed previously. The most sensitive effective parameters are those associated with the contribution of the $\Ar^{**}$ states and with the direct production of CF$_4$/CF$_3$ emitting species. In other words, the band width reflects the extent to which the secondary prediction depends on how populations simulated by Garfield++ are mapped onto genuinely scintillating populations.

The visible Ar--CF$_4$ comparison is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ArCF4_sec_VIS}.

For the visible and ultraviolet Ar--CF$_4$ predictions, as well as for the comparisons with He--CF$_4$, three reference curves are considered: the direct extrapolation of the primary parameters, the recommended curve and the curve providing the best joint description of primary and secondary data. The band is built from these three possibilities. This choice is more appropriate than a local statistical band because, in these regions, the dominant uncertainty is not solely associated with the numerical fit, but with the physical identification of which fraction of the $N_{\Ar^{**}}$, $N_{\CF_3^*}$ or $N_{\CF_4^{+,*}}$ populations actually contributes to the observed radiative channel. In the visible region, this identification can be carried out partially through energy thresholds, as discussed above. In the ultraviolet, however, the situation is more delicate, since a complete separation of the third-continuum precursor states and of $\CF_4^{+,*}$ states is not available in the cross sections used by Degrad or Garfield++.

The comparison with $\He-\CF_4$ is especially important. In this mixture, a wavelength-shifting mechanism equivalent to that of $\Ar-\CF_4$ is not expected, since helium does not efficiently feed the emitting species considered within the 200--800 nm interval. The observed emission must therefore originate essentially from the direct production of CF$_4$ species, mainly $\CF_4^{+,*}$ and $\CF_3^*$. The good agreement obtained for this mixture significantly strengthens the physical interpretation of the model: the direct CF$_4$ parameters do not appear to be merely effective fit parameters of the $\Ar-\CF_4$ mixture, but possess predictive power in a different base gas, with different cross sections, electron transport and energy distribution.

This result is one of the strongest points of the work. The $\He-\CF_4$ mixture provides an external validation of the model because it removes much of the complexity associated with argon transfers. If, despite this change, the prediction provides a reasonable description of secondary scintillation, the separation between microscopic production and radiative kinetics becomes more credible. In particular, it indicates that the model is not merely reproducing an empirical trend in $\Ar-\CF_4$, but is capturing a transferable component of the physics of CF$_4$ as a scintillating gas.

In the ultraviolet, the model qualitatively reproduces the scale and the general trend of the data, but clearer deviations appear, particularly at low pressure. This behaviour is expected. Ultraviolet emission involves several overlapping contributions: $\CF_4^{+,*}$ emission, the possible UV contribution of $\CF_3^*$, and the component associated with the argon third continuum. Unlike the visible region, where the 12.9 eV threshold provides a reasonable means of selecting the relevant $\Ar^{**}$ and $\CF_3^*$ excitations, no equivalent microscopic decomposition is available for all UV precursors. The model must therefore employ more global effective parameters.

At low pressure, moreover, the threshold-based scheme becomes less reliable. As pressure decreases, the mean free path increases and the way in which electrons gain energy between collisions changes. The avalanche-energy distribution may populate excited states differently from the regime in which the model was calibrated. Consequently, a fixed selection of states above a given threshold no longer represents correctly the true fraction of excitations that eventually produce light. This is probably the main reason why the UV prediction deteriorates in the low-pressure regime. It should not be interpreted as a global failure of the kinetic model, but as a specific limitation of the effective identification of emitting states when the microscopic avalanche distribution changes substantially.

The infrared prediction has a different nature. In the NIR, no parameters are retuned in order to describe the secondary data. The lines between 680 and 800 nm originate from atomic argon transitions, and the model employed is a direct extrapolation of the primary IR fit described in Section~\ref{subbsec:kinArIR}. Thus, the corresponding band is not built from the three parameter curves used in the visible and UV regions, but by propagating the statistical and systematic uncertainties of the primary fit to the secondary yield. This band is relatively small, especially on a logarithmic scale, because it does not include additional uncertainties associated with secondary experimental normalisation, gain or optical efficiency. It represents only the uncertainty inherited from the kinetic parameters of the primary IR model.

The good agreement in the infrared is therefore especially significant. Unlike visible $\Ar-\CF_4$ emission, for which adjustments related to the identification of $P_{\Ar^{**}}$ and of CF$_4$/CF$_3$ emitting populations are introduced, the NIR constitutes a much more direct prediction. The fact that both the absolute scale and the concentration dependence are reasonably reproduced suggests that the excited-argon populations simulated by Garfield++ and the radiative--collisional quenching model correctly capture the dominant physics. Furthermore, IR emission is particularly relevant at low CF$_4$ concentrations, where the mixture remains dominated by argon and the NIR lines may provide a non-negligible optical signal even before molecular emission from the additive reaches its maximum. This feature may be instrumentally useful, since it offers a spectral window complementary to visible and UV CF$_4$ emission.

\subsubsection{Dependence on Concentration and Pressure}

Once the overall scale of the model has been validated against experimental data, the dependence of secondary scintillation on CF$_4$ concentration and pressure is studied. Predictions are produced over the pressure interval from 0.05 to 10 bar for TH-GEM-like conditions and from 0.2 to 10 bar for GEM-like conditions. For both GEM and TH-GEM configurations, a fixed electron gain of 100 is chosen; that is, the electric field is calculated for each selected pressure and gap from a fit to Townsend coefficients obtained from simulations. This explains why the lowest-pressure GEM point is 0.2 bar rather than 0.05 bar: under the 0.05 bar conditions, no field yields a Townsend coefficient sufficient to reach a gain of 100 for a gap of 0.050 mm.

Representative GEM and TH-GEM conditions are therefore considered, including both visible and infrared contributions simultaneously. Figures~\ref{fig:GEM} and \ref{fig:th-GEM} make it possible to compare how the optical signal varies when the effective amplification geometry and the electric-field regime are modified.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{secondary_prediction/paper_v2_ArCF4_gem_VIS_IR.pdf}
\caption{Predicción del centelleo secundario visible e infrarrojo de $\Ar-\CF_4$ para condiciones representativas de una geometría tipo GEM. La banda refleja la variación asociada a los parámetros efectivos $P_{\Ar^{**}}$ y a la producción directa de especies emisoras de CF$_4$/CF$_3$.}
\label{fig:GEM}
\end{figure}


In GEM-like geometries, the amplification region is short and the electric field required to reach appreciable gain is high. Under these conditions, excitation production is concentrated over a short distance. The visible signal increases clearly when small amounts of CF$_4$ are introduced, owing both to transfer from excited argon states and to the direct production of CF$_4$ species. At higher concentrations, however, competing mechanisms arise: quenching, attachment and redistribution of electron energy modify the efficiency of light production. The optical yield therefore does not increase indefinitely with concentration, but instead presents optimal regions.

Pressure introduces an equally important effect. Within the operating ranges considered, higher pressures may favour greater optical production per avalanche electron. For comparable gain and geometry conditions, the effective reduced field $E/p$ may lie in a region where the frequency of exciting collisions increases relative to an excessively energetic regime. In simple terms, increasing the pressure increases the number of molecules available per unit length and reduces the mean distance between collisions. If the field remains sufficient to sustain the avalanche, this higher collisional density can result in more excitations and therefore more photons per electron. This behaviour is particularly attractive for rare-event detectors, in which the optical signal should be maximised without driving the structure towards discharge or instability regimes.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{secondary_prediction/paper_v2_ArCF4_thgem_VIS_IR.pdf}
\caption{Predicción del centelleo secundario visible e infrarrojo de $\Ar-\CF_4$ para condiciones representativas de una geometría tipo TH-GEM. La mayor longitud efectiva de avalancha favorece un incremento del rendimiento óptico frente a estructuras más finas.}
\label{fig:th-GEM}
\end{figure}


For a TH-GEM-like geometry, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:th-GEM}, the increased thickness relative to a conventional GEM allows the avalanche to develop over a more extended region and therefore increases the accumulated number of collisions per electron. This is reflected in a greater expected optical yield. From the detector-design point of view, this result is highly relevant: achieving high electron gain is not the only important requirement; producing the largest possible amount of light per amplified electron is equally important. Two structures with similar gain may have different optical yields when the microscopic histories of their avalanches are not the same.

This observation is particularly suggestive for FAT-GEMs. Since these structures are even thicker, can operate at more moderate fields and provide amplification regions several millimetres long, it is reasonable to expect still greater optical production, provided that gain remains stable and that losses due to attachment, excessive quenching or discharges do not dominate. In this respect, the model indicates that thick structures may be particularly interesting candidates for optical readout in gaseous TPCs, where an intense, extended light signal compatible with external sensors is sought.

In the visible predictions, the bands retain the same interpretation as in the initial comparison with data: they reflect the uncertainty between effective-parameter schemes, mainly those related to $P_{\Ar^{**}}$ and to the direct production of CF$_4$/CF$_3$ emitting species. The band should therefore not be interpreted as a local statistical fluctuation, but as the physical variation associated with identifying which fractions of the simulated populations are genuinely radiative in the secondary regime. The IR contribution, by contrast, behaves as a more direct prediction, since it arises from parameters fitted in the primary regime and propagated to the secondary regime without an additional fit.

An important result of these predictions is the role of infrared emission at low CF$_4$ concentration. In this region, the mixture is still dominated by argon and the NIR lines retain an appreciable contribution. As the CF$_4$ concentration increases, visible and UV molecular emissions gain importance, but quenching of the argon states responsible for IR emission also increases. NIR light therefore appears to be an especially useful signal in mixtures with a low additive fraction, whereas visible CF$_4$ emission dominates in regions where transfer and direct production of molecular species are more efficient. This spectral complementarity may be very valuable for optical detectors, since it allows the readout window to be selected according to the mixture, pressure and amplification structure.

\subsubsection{Dependence on Concentration and Electric Field}

Figure~\ref{fig:ArCF4_sec_vsE_1percentCF4} shows the dependence of visible and infrared yield on electric field at a fixed CF$_4$ concentration of 1\%. This type of representation is particularly useful because it directly relates the model to an operating variable of the detector. While concentration and pressure determine gas composition and density, the electric field determines the mean energy acquired by electrons between collisions and therefore the relative probabilities of excitation, ionisation and attachment.

At low fields, the energy acquired by electrons may be insufficient to populate efficiently the excited states responsible for emission. As the field increases, the probability of exceeding excitation and ionisation thresholds grows, so that both gain and the number of photons per electron may increase significantly. This growth, however, should not be interpreted as unlimited. At very high fields, an increasing fraction of electron energy may be directed towards ionisation, attachment or non-radiative channels, and the electrical stability of the structure becomes more critical. The most interesting instrumental region is therefore not necessarily the maximum-field regime, but rather the region in which optical production is high while detector operation remains stable.

For mixtures containing small concentrations of CF$_4$, the competition between visible and IR emission is particularly illustrative. The visible region benefits from the presence of the molecular additive and from transfer from excited argon states, whereas IR emission more directly reflects the radiative survival of excited atomic argon states. Changing the electric field consequently modifies the balance between both spectral windows. This opens the possibility of optimising a detector not only in terms of electron gain, but also in terms of the spectral composition of the emitted light.

\subsubsection{Physical Discussion and Instrumental Implications}

The secondary-scintillation results show remarkable agreement with the available data, especially considering that a large fraction of the predictions are obtained from parameters fitted in the primary regime and microscopic populations independently simulated with Garfield++. This point is central: the model is not limited to interpolating secondary data, but attempts to predict them from a common physical description of excited-state production and subsequent kinetic evolution.

Agreement is particularly strong in two cases. First, in $\He-\CF_4$, where the prediction is based on direct CF$_4$ emission rather than on argon-transfer mechanisms. The reasonable reproduction of the data indicates that the direct parameters associated with CF$_4$ possess predictive ability beyond the mixture in which they were determined. Second, in the infrared emission of $\Ar-\CF_4$, where the prediction is obtained without fitting parameters specifically to secondary data. In this case, the good description of both scale and concentration dependence supports the validity of the radiative--collisional treatment of argon NIR lines.

The visible $\Ar-\CF_4$ case is also highly positive, although it is conceptually more dependent on the effective-parameter scheme. The need to consider several curves---direct, recommended and optimal---should not be understood as an arbitrary weakness, but as an honest representation of the physical uncertainty associated with identifying the emitting populations. The available cross sections do not always separate excited states with the degree of detail required by a complete kinetic model. Parameters such as $P_{\Ar^{**}}$ and the direct probabilities associated with CF$_4$/CF$_3$ therefore contain both a radiative probability and a fraction of the states that are genuinely involved. The corresponding prediction bands reflect precisely this limitation.

The ultraviolet region is the most delicate case. Although the model reproduces the general trend, agreement deteriorates at low pressure. This deviation is consistent with the physical interpretation of the model: the threshold scheme used to select relevant populations is no longer sufficiently robust when the electron-energy distribution in the avalanche changes substantially. In addition, UV emission combines contributions from several species and precursors whose microscopic separation is not fully available in Garfield++ or Magboltz. The UV prediction should therefore be interpreted as a reasonable estimate in the principal operating regime, rather than as a universal extrapolation to any pressure or electric field.

From an instrumental point of view, the predictions clearly support the interest of $\Ar-\CF_4$ mixtures for optical readout in MPGDs. At low CF$_4$ concentrations, an important argon infrared contribution is retained, while at moderate concentrations intense visible emission associated with $\CF_3^*$ and transfer processes appears. This combination provides considerable flexibility: the detector can exploit different spectral windows according to the available photosensor, operating pressure and amplification geometry. Furthermore, the expected increase in optical yield in thicker structures, such as TH-GEMs and possibly FAT-GEMs, indicates a clear route for optimisation in optical TPCs and rare-event searches, where each additional photon may improve spatial reconstruction, effective threshold and topological-discrimination capability.

\section{Conclusions}

In this work, a phenomenological model has been developed to describe and predict primary and secondary scintillation in gas mixtures relevant to optical detectors based on MPGDs. The adopted approach separates two distinct physical ingredients: the microscopic production of excited and ionised populations, obtained using Degrad or Garfield++, and the subsequent kinetic evolution of these populations, governed by radiative emission, quenching, energy transfer and relaxation processes. This separation allows the same physical framework to be used in studying both primary emission, expressed in ph/MeV, and secondary avalanche emission, expressed in ph/e$^-$.

The $\Ar-\CF_4$ mixture has been the principal system in this study. Three especially relevant spectral regions have been identified: visible emission from $\CF_3^*$, UV emission associated with $\CF_4^{+,*}$, $\CF_3^*$ and the argon third continuum, and the argon NIR lines between 680 and 800 nm. The model satisfactorily reproduces the primary data and allows extrapolation to secondary emission with a notable degree of agreement. This result is especially significant because the secondary response is not fitted from scratch: it is predicted using Garfield++ populations and parameters obtained in the primary regime, with additional adjustments only where the identification of excited states requires effective parameters to be introduced.

One of the most important results is the description of secondary scintillation in $\He-\CF_4$. This mixture acts as an independent test of the model, since it does not exhibit the energy-transfer mechanisms characteristic of $\Ar-\CF_4$ in the spectral interval considered. The good agreement obtained indicates that the direct parameters associated with CF$_4$ are not merely empirical parameters of the argon mixture, but retain predictive capability in a different base gas. This supports the interpretation that the model reasonably captures the intrinsic physics of the CF$_4$ emitting species.

A second particularly robust result is the prediction of infrared secondary scintillation in $\Ar-\CF_4$. In this case, no secondary-specific adjustments have been introduced: the uncertainty band derives directly from propagation of the statistical and systematic uncertainties of the primary IR fit. Agreement with secondary data therefore provides strong validation of the radiative--collisional treatment of argon lines and of the populations simulated by Garfield++. Moreover, IR emission emerges as a particularly relevant optical window at low CF$_4$ concentrations, where the mixture remains dominated by argon and NIR lines may provide an appreciable signal.

Visible $\Ar-\CF_4$ emission also displays very satisfactory agreement, although its interpretation depends more strongly on the effective parameters $P_{\Ar^{**}}$ and on the direct probabilities associated with CF$_4$/CF$_3$ species. The bands shown in the visible and UV predictions do not represent only the statistical uncertainty of the fit, but the variation among three physical schemes: direct primary extrapolation, the recommended curve and the optimal curve for the joint description of primary and secondary signals. This representation honestly reflects the main limitation of the model: the available cross sections do not always make it possible to separate every precursor excited state with the required detail.

The ultraviolet region is the most challenging. Although the model reasonably describes the overall trend, the scheme ceases to work correctly at low pressure. The most likely cause is the loss of validity of threshold-based identification when the electron-energy distribution in the avalanche changes. In this regime, electrons travel longer free paths, gain energy differently and populate excited states with a distribution different from that assumed by the effective model. The UV prediction should therefore be regarded as reliable in the main operating range, but should not be extrapolated without caution to very low pressures.

From an instrumental perspective, the predictions show that $\Ar-\CF_4$ mixtures are highly promising candidates for optical readout in MPGDs. The coexistence of visible, UV and infrared emission enables the spectral window to be adapted to the available photosensor and operating conditions. Furthermore, the study of GEM- and TH-GEM-like geometries indicates that thicker structures may favour greater light production per avalanche electron by increasing the effective amplification length and the number of exciting collisions. This suggests that TH-GEMs and, of particular interest, FAT-GEMs could be highly suitable geometries for maximising secondary scintillation in optical gaseous detectors.

Overall, the level of agreement reached for secondary scintillation, particularly in the IR emission of $\Ar-\CF_4$ and in $\He-\CF_4$, constitutes one of the most important validations of this work. In both cases, the predictions are close to first-principles predictions within the phenomenological framework employed: simulated microscopic populations are combined with kinetic parameters extracted from the primary regime, without freely refitting the secondary response. These results therefore support the central idea of this thesis: predictive scintillation models can be built for gas mixtures, connecting the microphysics of electron--gas collisions with optical observables relevant to real detectors.

As future work, it would be especially important to improve differential cross sections for individual excited states in both CF$_4$ and argon, so that effective parameters could progressively be replaced by better identified microscopic populations. Extending the simulations to realistic non-uniform field geometries, incorporating optical-transport effects and comparing with new absolute secondary-scintillation measurements in TH-GEMs and FAT-GEMs would also be valuable. Despite these limitations, the model developed here provides a useful tool for optimising mixtures, pressures, electric fields and amplification geometries in gaseous detectors with optical readout.
